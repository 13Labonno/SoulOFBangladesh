The Chakma

The Chakma

The Chakma constitute the largest ethnic group in the CHT, primarily residing in the Rangamati and Khagrachhari districts. They are predominantly practitioners of Theravada Buddhism and are culturally distinct, possessing their own script (Ojhopath) and a rich literary tradition. Their traditional attire is famous: women wear the woven wrap-around lower garment known as the Pinoon and the upper piece called the Khadi, reflecting their skill in intricate weaving.

The Marma

Marma

The Marma are the second-largest group, heavily concentrated in the Bandarban, Khagrachhari, and Rangamati regions. Like the Chakma, the Marma are Buddhist and share cultural ties with the Rakhine people. They are known for their festive celebrations, especially their water-throwing festival, Sangrai, which marks the traditional new year. Their culture is rich in distinctive traditional music and their expertise in bamboo and wood craftsmanship is widely recognized.

The Tripura

Tripura

The Tripura are one of the oldest inhabitants of the CHT, found mainly in the Khagrachhari and Bandarban districts. They practice a mix of Hinduism and traditional animistic beliefs, worshipping various gods of nature. The most prominent cultural expression of the Tripura is their lively Garia Dance, performed during the harvest festival to honor the deity of the same name. They are also known for their unique skills in bamboo and cane work used in everyday life.

The Mro

Mro

The Mro (or Mru) community primarily resides in the remote, forested areas of Bandarban. They are notable for maintaining a relatively isolated culture, speaking the Mro language which is sometimes written in their own unique script. Their traditional religion is often a blend of indigenous animism and spiritual beliefs, characterized by rituals centered on nature and ancestors. They are easily distinguishable by their distinct housing style and are highly skilled forest dwellers.

The Tanchangya

Tanchangya

The Tanchangya are culturally and linguistically similar to the Chakma but maintain their own unique identity, often noted for their intricate traditional jewelry.

The Bawm

Bawm

The Bawm are largely Christian and live in the higher, remote hills of Bandarban, known for their specific dialects and the colorful, woven shawls and fabrics made by the women.

The Garo

Garo

The Garo are a distinguished ethnic group in Bangladesh's northern districts (Mymensingh, Netrokona, etc.). They speak the Tibeto-Burman A'chik language and are defined by a strict matriarchal system of lineage and property. The Garo are largely Christian, retain animistic roots, and celebrate the harvest festival, Wangaala.

The Rakhaine

Rakhaine

The Rakhine, descendants of Arakanese immigrants, reside in the coastal districts of Cox's Bazar and Patuakhali (Kuakata). They are devout followers of Theravada Buddhism and are known for their magnificent monasteries (Kyangs) and expertise in traditional handloom weaving

Bizu Utsob

Bizu Utsob is the most significant socio-cultural festival for the indigenous communities of Bangladesh, particularly the Jummo people residing in the Chittagong Hill Tracts. This festival marks the traditional New Year, celebrated concurrently by the Chakma (Bizu), Marma (Sangrai), and Tripura (Bisu) communities, occurring around mid-April, aligning with the Bengali New Year.

Pahari Bazar

The "Pahari Bazar" (which literally means "Hill Market") is a central institution in the cultural and economic life of the indigenous communities, particularly the Jummo people, in the Chittagong Hill Tracts.
